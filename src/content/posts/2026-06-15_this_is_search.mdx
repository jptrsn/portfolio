## The problem

Back in March, I took delivery of some new microcontroller development kits, the ESP32 S3 Super Mini! I wanted to put some firmware on them (specifically, WLED), but the stock binary doesn't include the correct partition tables or memory mapping, so the general solution that's a "one-size-fits-all" doesn't fit here.

tl;dr - I needed to figure out something where most of the info online does not apply to my specific case.

## Round One, Fight!

I turned to my favourite tool, web search, to look for information and to find out who had experienced this problem, and how they'd solved it. Web forums, stack overflow, the manufacturer's web page, the firmware developer's website, all those resources turned up a lot of information, but nothing specific to the problem I was experiencing.

So I turned to my favourite search provider's new, latest and greatest tech, their chatbot, Gemini. I set the model to their "fast" version and asked it for help. After a few back-and-forth messages, I finally told it to do a web search and tell me what it found. That was the prompt that managed to identify the solution. [You can view the full prompt history here](https://gemini.google.com/share/dc70297260d1).

### That's Just Web Search with Extra Steps

So far, so okay. It didn't work, but telling it to look for info actually yielded results. A decade ago, I likely would have been able to find those same results myself because the information would be much more visible and accessible to the web crawlers of the time. The important thing is that I managed to get the problem solved, even though it took a little bit of work to prompt the token generator to give me the helpful output.

## Round Two...

Unfortunately, as research has shown, when I use an LLM in this way, I tend not to retain information as effectively [source](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.04167). This meant that, a few days later when I wanted to do the same thing, I went back to the method that I had used the first time: ask Gemini.

This time, however, I was unable to get the same level of help. Instead of providing me with the same solution as it had previously, the LLM took me in circles.

Initially, I asked it to check our conversation history, in order to surface the solution we had identified. I knew where the information was, and I figured that asking a tool published by the premier search platform on the internet to search through a set of data it created with me in order to find the useful information was a straightfoward task.

### Wrong Off The Bat

The first prompt I entered felt simple and clear - "Can you check our conversation history, I think you helped me get WLED running on my esp32 s3 super mini."

You'd think that prompt was a request for the chatbot to review the conversation history. I sure did.

That's not what happened.

### Model Training Data everywhere

The first response contained factually false information.

"Once that was flashed, the blinking blue LED confirmed the device had entered Access Point mode, though we also noted that the LED was tied to the battery charging circuit, which was causing the board to run a bit warm."

That's just not true, and was never a part of the original conversation I asked it about. My particular board has a blinking blue LED that is only related to the on-board battery charge circuit. Most information online about any blue blinking LED is actually about AP mode, but in my case, that's not even possible.

### Distractions, Distractions

Helpful, I'm sure, but not helpful to me. Not asked for, not wanted, not helpful.

## What Did We Learn?

No matter how many times you might prompt them to search, LLMs are not search. In fact, it turns out that Gemini preferred to lie to me than to follow explicit directions in a prompt.

This brings me a lot of concern, and it should be obvious why.

### Outweighed By Training

In this specific scenario, I had enough experience and prior knowledge to push back and identify when the LLM was bullshitting. In this use-case, the amount was astonishing. The LLM appeared to prioritize its training data over the specific directions in the prompt, and when I refuted the information it provided, the next tokens it generated were always a plausible-sounding rationalization of its falsehoods.

### Evade Accountability

Not only did Gemini not follow direct instructions, the model had several plausible explanations ready to justify its bullshit. This is not because of any "decision" - the model has been tuned to do this. Intentionally. When provided with a direct contradiction to any information it output, Gemini found a multitude of ways to assign the blame to a human. Ways that were plausible but impossible. A series of tokens to shift responsibility subtly away from its own failings as an architecture.

### Is This Your Future Colleague?

Gemini has to be the worst "colleague" I could imagine. Confident, knowledgeable, and consistently wrong without ever appearing to incorporate feedback. This is the vision for the future these LLM providers want to create. If I were working with a person who did this, they would be on a performance improvement plan before the end of the day, and likely would be fired before the end of the quarter. If I were the one responsible for paying the cost of these tools, I would be very carefully eyeing the amount of venture capital that is currently subsidising the cost of these low-quality tools.

### We Can't Have Nice things

As LLMs continue to undermine the current state of web, it's going to become more commonplace to encounter this kind of obfuscation and frustration. Search tools are no longer as useful, and the large language models that are replacing them are built on a foundational misapplication of technology. A generative pre-trained transformer generates the next token in a sequence. An incredibly complex sequence, to be sure, but that's what it does, and that's how it works, at a fundamental level.

### Pay to Play

I'm expecting to hear a lot of feedback that I shouldn't be using the free models. I understand, their utility is limited. However, if I'm attempting to perform a search function, I would expect the flagship product from the industry leader in the web search space that reached its position of market dominance though fast, free, accurate search to provide a similar experience.

The plain truth is this: if I'm forced to pay, I sure as heck am not going to be paying the corporation that has reduced search to this. [Kagi](https://kagi.com/) are far more likely to get my business, since they appear to be offering the tool I'm looking for.
